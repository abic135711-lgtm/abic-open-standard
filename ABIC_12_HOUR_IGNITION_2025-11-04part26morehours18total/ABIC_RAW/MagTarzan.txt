✅ BOOK I — THE SPARK OF POSSIBILITY
The birth of a vision. The fight to prove it works.

A roadmap hidden inside a novel.

✅ ACT I — The Idea That Changes Everything
Chapter 1 — The Problem No One Sees

Setting: Near-future Earth (5–15 years from present).
Protagonist: A mid-career aerospace engineer / physicist (your choice of specialty later).

Core beats:

Introduce his frustration: We have the tech to go to the Belt, so why aren’t we using it?

He watches yet another Mars mission concept get canceled for cost.

He runs the numbers and realizes the cost killer: lifting mass from Earth.

He asks the question that becomes the thesis of the trilogy:

“What if we moved mass through space the way nature already moves planets—by trading momentum, not burning propellant?”

First spark of the system.

Chapter 2 — Emergence of the Mag-Tarzan Idea

He synthesizes three mature technologies:

Electromagnetic launch systems

Electrodynamic tethers

Iron-rich asteroids

The key insight:
Using magnetic grapples + coilguns, a tender ship can “swing” between iron-rich bodies the way Tarzan swings between branches.

He runs feasibility simulations:

power demands

anchor spacing

orbital mechanics

safety factors

energy cost per Δv

It works on paper.

He thinks it’s crazy no one has connected these dots.

He drafts a white paper.

Chapter 3 — The Anchor-Buoy Network

He expands the idea:

small robotic buoys placed around iron-rich asteroids

each is a “grab point” for EM harpoons

powered by RTGs or solar-nuclear hybrid

form a momentum-trading network

He realizes this could also support:

navigation

mapping

mining support

tender guidance

traffic control

He writes that this solves the propellant problem inside the Belt.

Chapter 4 — Cans and Corridors

The CanCorridor is conceived:

standardized cargo cylinders (“cans”)

launched by a mass driver

on a fixed arc

to a known catcher corridor

caught by a momentum-exchange tether near the Moon or L1 depot

Realistic engineering:

2–10 ton payloads

containerized resource shipping

low-maintenance, high cadence

electricity-based, not propellant-based

Now he sees the chain:
Asteroid → tender → Ark → can → mass driver → cis-lunar depot → Earth/Near-Earth uses

Chapter 5 — The Belt Ark Concept

Not a starship — a construction/processing habitat:

spin gravity

thick water shielding

nuclear power plant

life support eco-loop

hangar bays for tenders

solar farms + radiator wings

hydroponics

robotic foundry bays

He realizes:
We don’t need to go to Mars first. We need to go to the Belt.

And we can do it in pieces:

launch parts to ISS (or successor platform)

assemble in orbit

minimal new tech required

Chapter 6 — First Rejection

He submits the white paper to NASA / ESA / JAXA / NSF / major universities.

Responses:

“Too ambitious.”

“Too dangerous.”

“Too politically volatile.”

“We don’t have the budget.”

“It’s unrealistic without fusion.”

“We can’t do this.”

He’s devastated.
But one person pays attention.

✅ ACT II — The Allies Arrive
Chapter 7 — The Unexpected Ally

A senior orbital mechanics expert or retired astronaut reaches out.

They say:

“It’s wild. Which means it’s exactly what the field needs.”

They introduce him to a small network of:

engineers

roboticists

nuclear specialists

materials scientists

This is the seed of the coalition.

Chapter 8 — The Private Sector Crack

He gets a meeting with a private firm (SpaceX-analog, Blue Origin-analog, or new player).

They’re interested in:

reusable cis-lunar infrastructure

in-space construction

asteroid resource positioning

They want proof of concept.

They fund a small orbital test.

Chapter 9 — Prototype #1: Failure That Proves It's Possible

Realistic 1st test:

A CubeSat-sized EM harpoon

Fired at a small iron-rich debris test target

Attached to a tether

With mini-coil assist

What happens:

The tether overheats

The harpoon misses its anchor

The system half-fails

But the telemetry proves:

the math is sound

the energy transfer is valid

the concept works

A failure that’s actually a victory.

They gain credibility.

Chapter 10 — The Engineering Consortium

A Japanese robotics team joins (for precision anchoring).

A French orbital dynamics group joins (for tether modeling).

A Canadian mining automation company joins.

A small nuclear startup joins (for MW-class space reactors).

The ecosystem is forming.

Chapter 11 — Political Pushback

Russia claims resource theft

Lobbyists from:

fossil fuel

terrestrial mining

aerospace incumbents
…start interfering.

Not malicious — just threatened.

The protagonist must learn politics.

Chapter 12 — The Breakthrough Model

A young researcher or grad student solves:

the tether heating problem

the coil-timing issue

or the anchor-grapple stability equations

A small adjustment unlocks:

safe EM grapples

sustainable tender “swing” maneuvers

the full Mag-Tarzan system

This is a watershed moment.

Chapter 13 — Proof-of-Concept #2 (The Demonstration)

They launch a second orbital test

This time:

a stable tether

successful harpoon lock

energy-trading maneuver

safe orbital reposition

The world takes notice.
A billionaire philanthropist offers funding.
A European director proposes partnership.
The media starts asking questions.

The protagonist becomes a public figure — reluctantly.
