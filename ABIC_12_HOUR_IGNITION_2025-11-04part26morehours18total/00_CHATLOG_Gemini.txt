# 1) Magnetic grapple physics: range, force, heating
**Why it can fail:** fields fall fast; eddy current heating; quench limits.
* **Field energy density / pressure:** (P \approx \dfrac{B^2}{2\mu_0}).
  At **3 T** → (P \approx 3.6) MPa → **MN-class** force only at tight coupling (~cm).
* **Range law:** force from magnet on ferromagnet scales roughly (\propto 1/r^{4}) (dipole–image idealization). **Implication:** meaningful EM force only within **decimeters**. Beyond that, use **mechanical harpoon + winch**; the coil gives short pulses and clamping.
* **Skin depth (eddy heating):** (\delta=\sqrt{\tfrac{2\rho}{\mu\omega}}).
  Iron: (\rho\sim10^{-7}\ \Omega!·\text{m}), (\mu_r\gg1). At kHz, (\delta) is **mm-scale** ⇒ strong surface currents/heat.
  **Mitigate** with **DC/low-freq** pulls and short pulses; avoid continuous HF fields.
* **Superconductor limits:** practical steady (B)(\lesssim) 5–10 T (NbTi/Nb3Sn/HTS), with **pulsed** higher. Quench if coil sees too much AC loss or thermal spike.
  **Red line:** if you *need* >~5 T steady at >0.5 m standoff for kN forces, **doesn’t pencil**. Use tethers.
**Pass condition:** Treat EM as a near-contact clamp/impulse; do the real work with **triangulated tethers/winches**.
---
# 2) Anchor mass, cohesion & “don’t move the rock”
**Why it can fail:** you tug a small boulder → you just meet in the middle; or you rip regolith.
* **Momentum exchange:** ship impulse (J = m_s\Delta v). Equal/opposite to anchor: (\Delta V_a = J/M_a).
  For (m_s=10^5) kg, (\Delta v=10) m/s → (J=10^6\ \text{N·s}).
  With (M_a=10^{11}) kg → (\Delta V_a = 10^{-5}) m/s (negligible).
  **Red line:** if (M_a \lesssim 10^{8}) kg (≈30-m rock), your pull **repositions the anchor** too much.
* **Regolith/aggregate shear:** rubble-pile cohesion σ(*c) can be **10–1000 Pa** (wide!).
  Permissible tether tension (F*\text{max} \approx \sigma_c A_\text{embed}).
  With three darts each embedding **0.05 m²** equivalent and σ(_c)=300 Pa → per-dart ~15 kN, tripod ≈45 kN.
  **Design:** keep steady pulls **≤ 50 kN** per contact, add **pitons/drilled anchors** on metal.
  **Red line:** trying 100–300 kN on loose regolith without proper anchors = **rips out**.
**Pass condition:** choose anchors **≫ ship mass** and use **multiple penetrators** (or embed plates on metallic bodies).
---
# 3) Tether capture dynamics: strain & timing
**Why it can fail:** over-G at capture, material strain, missed timing.
* **Strain energy:** for tether leg with modulus (E) and cross-section (A), max strain (\varepsilon_\text{max} = \sigma_\text{allow}/E).
  HMPE (Dyneema): (E\sim100) GPa, allow. stress (SF 4) ≈ **0.8–1.0 GPa** ⇒ (\varepsilon\sim0.8–1%).
* **Stopping distance:** (x \approx \dfrac{m_c v^2}{2 A \sigma_\text{eff}}).
  Example: (m_c=2000) kg, (v=10) m/s, (A=4\ \text{cm}^2), (\sigma_\text{eff}=0.5) GPa →
  (x \approx (2000·100)/(2·4e-4·5e8)) = (200,000 / (400,000)) ≈ **0.5 m** (OK).
  **Red line:** high (v) catches (>~40 m/s) or skinny lines cause **snap** or **excess stroke**.
* **Timing tolerance:** tip speed (v_\text{tip}=3000) m/s, timing error (\Delta t). Miss distance (d\approx v_\text{tip}\Delta t).
  For (d\le3) m window → (\Delta t\le1) ms.
  **Red line:** guidance/clock sync worse than **millisecond** scale at capture ⇒ **miss**.
  (**Mitigate** with phased arrays, retroreflectors, LIDAR, Kalman filtering.)
**Pass condition:** keep capture relative speeds to **≤10–20 m/s**, use **elastic stroke** in the grapple, and hold **ms-class timing**.

# 4) Mass driver: acceleration loads & power
**Why it can fail:** cans crush; power spikes unrealistic.
* Kinematics: (a = v^2/(2L)).
  For (v=2000) m/s:
  • (L=1000) m → (a=2000) m/s² ≈ **200 g**
  • (L=5000) m → (a=400) m/s² ≈ **40 g**
  • (L=10,000) m → **20 g**
* **Red line:** If your payloads can’t live at **20–50 g axial**, you need **long barrels** or **multistage accel**.
* **Energy/ton:** ideal (E=\tfrac12 m v^2) → **0.56 MWh/t** at 2 km/s; at 30–50% total efficiency ⇒ **1.1–1.9 MWh/t** electrical.

# 6) Thermal control: radiators
**Why it can fail:** nowhere to dump the waste heat.

PART I — GENERAL PROVISIONS
Article 1 — Definitions
For the purposes of this Convention:

   2. “CanCorridor” means a designated transport lane for standardized cargo containers propelled through space by non-rocket energy systems.
   3. “Container” or “Cargo Canister” means any ABIC-certified vessel designed to carry bulk materials or equipment through a CanCorridor.
   4. “Depot” means any orbital facility certified under this Convention for cargo capture, processing, storage, refueling, or redistribution.
   5. “Rotovator” means a tether-based momentum-exchange system used for capturing and redirecting cargo canisters.
   6. “Operator” means any public or private entity authorized by a Member State and licensed by the IFOC to operate ABIC infrastructure or resource extraction activities.
   7. “Extraction Activity” means any drilling, excavation, sample return, processing, or transfer of material from an asteroid or related celestial body.
   8. “Managed Commons” refers to the legal status under which asteroid resources are not subject to national appropriation, but extraction is permitted through regulated, licensed activity under this Convention.
   9. “Member State” means any nation that has ratified or acceded to this Convention.

   12. “Nuclear Power Unit (NPU)” means any fission-based reactor deployed in space for ABIC operations.
   13. “Scientific Preservation Zone” means a region of a celestial body designated by the IFOC for restricted or prohibited activity.

PREAMBLE
The Parties to this Convention,
Recognizing the growing importance of safe, sustainable, and cooperative access to outer space for the economic, scientific, and technological advancement of all humankind;

PART I — GENERAL PROVISIONS
Article 1 — Definitions
For the purposes of this Convention:
   1. “ABIC” means the Asteroid Belt Industrial Corridor, a coordinated system of orbital depots, containerized transport lanes, electromagnetic launchers, tether- and rotovator-based capture systems, and associated infrastructure used to transport mass within the solar system.
   2. “CanCorridor” means a designated transport lane for standardized cargo containers propelled through space by non-rocket energy systems.
   3. “Container” or “Cargo Canister” means any ABIC-certified vessel designed to carry bulk materials or equipment through a CanCorridor.
   4. “Depot” means any orbital facility certified under this Convention for cargo capture, processing, storage, refueling, or redistribution.
   5. “Rotovator” means a tether-based momentum-exchange system used for capturing and redirecting cargo canisters.
   6. “Operator” means any public or private entity authorized by a Member State and licensed by the IFOC to operate ABIC infrastructure or resource extraction activities.
   7. “Extraction Activity” means any drilling, excavation, sample return, processing, or transfer of material from an asteroid or related celestial body.
   8. “Managed Commons” refers to the legal status under which asteroid resources are not subject to national appropriation, but extraction is permitted through regulated, licensed activity under this Convention.
   9. “Member State” means any nation that has ratified or acceded to this Convention.
   10. “IFOC” means the International Freight Operations Consortium established under Article 5 of this Convention.
   11. “Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA)” means a formal review process evaluating the effects of proposed extraction activities on the integrity, stability, and scientific value of a celestial body.
   12. “Nuclear Power Unit (NPU)” means any fission-based reactor deployed in space for ABIC operations.
   13. “Scientific Preservation Zone” means a region of a celestial body designated by the IFOC for restricted or prohibited activity.
Article 2 — Scope and Application
   1. This Convention applies to:
   * (a) all ABIC infrastructure deployed in any orbit or trajectory;
   * (b) all extraction and processing of resources from asteroids;
   * (c) all CanCorridors, depots, rotovators, and tenders used as part of ABIC operations;
   * (d) all nuclear power systems used to support ABIC logistics;
   * (e) all private operators authorized by Member States.
   2. This Convention does not supersede the Outer Space Treaty or existing binding obligations under international law.
   3. Activities carried out by private operators remain the responsibility of their licensing Member State under international law.
Article 3 — Peaceful Use and Non-Weaponization
   1. ABIC infrastructure shall be used exclusively for peaceful purposes.
   2. No Member State or Operator may:
   * (a) deploy weapons within ABIC infrastructure;
   * (b) modify ABIC systems to deliver kinetic or energy-based destructive force;
   * (c) use ABIC infrastructure to interfere with the space assets of other States.
   3. Dual-use technologies must be subject to transparency and inspection requirements to ensure peaceful intent.
Article 4 — Legal Status of Asteroids and Extracted Resources
   1. Celestial bodies, including asteroids, shall not be subject to national appropriation by claim of sovereignty.
   2. Extraction of resources is permitted only under:
   * (a) a valid license issued by the IFOC, and 
   * (b) compliance with safety, environmental, and transparency requirements.
   3. Resources extracted under a valid license become the property of the Operator, subject to:
   * (a) reporting requirements;
   * (b) benefit-sharing obligations;
   * (c) environmental and scientific restrictions.
   4. No Operator may alter the orbital dynamics of a celestial body without explicit authorization from the IFOC.
   5. The IFOC may designate Scientific Preservation Zones in which extraction is restricted or prohibited.
